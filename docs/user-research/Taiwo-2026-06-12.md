### Onboarding

#### [HIGH · New feature / Copy] No state between payment and first proposal

> "My point now is that I paid $10 to you and I can't really do anything with the platform." (00:44:44)
> "So really there's nothing much I can do right now." (00:50:54)

**What's happening:** The gap between subscribing and receiving a first trade proposal can be hours or days. During that window, the app has nothing for the user to act on. The signals page shows signals but they require explanation. The daily brief exists but is not surfaced as the primary "here is what is happening right now" entry point. The user correctly perceives they've paid for a service and received nothing actionable yet.

Henry named this directly: "that's the biggest like product thing I need to solve is the distance between what happens between when I subscribe and my first trade proposal. Every onboarding call I've done at this point is just people like, 'Okay, so what happens now? What do I do now?'" (00:52:10)

**Why it matters:** First-hour experience determines whether users churn before the product can deliver value. Every prior onboarding call has hit this same wall; it is not a Taiwo-specific issue.

**Suggested action:** Build an explicit "waiting for your first signal" state on the home page after onboarding. Show what the system is currently monitoring, when it last ran, and what will happen when a signal fires. A countdown to the next signal check run, or the most recent brief, would give users something concrete to read while waiting.

**What's happening:** Taiwo arrived with the mental model of a trading interface where he initiates trades. **Why it matters:** A user who thinks they can place trades will be confused and frustrated for their entire first session. If they do not receive a verbal correction (as in an onboarding call), they will likely churn thinking the platform is broken.

Proposals are the core UX; they should be described before the user ever reaches the signals list.

### Account (Deposit)

#### [HIGH · Behavior] Deposit flow took 30 minutes on a live guided call — unsupported users would abandon

> "I don't have money on them. I've never used IP liquid like that." (00:09:37 — referring to Hyperliquid)
> "So how was this how was this money sent? Money was sent from this to this. How..." (00:25:23)
> "I was asking about the breed because if he uses this they nonsense native bridge like the one we're using." (00:25:23)

**What's happening:** Taiwo had ETH in a personal wallet. The actual path to deposit was: sell ETH for USDC → withdraw USDC to Arbitrum → wait for auto-bridge cron (10-min cycle) to sweep Arbitrum USDC to Hyperliquid → wait for agent registration cron to fire. The entire flow took approximately 30 minutes during the call, required Henry to manually inspect Arbitrum block explorers, identify the USDC contract variant (native vs. bridge), and ultimately trigger the sweep manually via Claude Code.

Henry: "This takes too long. This is the longest sweep I've seen before." (00:25:23)

Even with Henry on the call, Taiwo said: "the difference between non technical people. I would have just logged into the server and just run the chrome job." (00:14:51)

**Why it matters:** A solo user without the founder on a call would have no way to debug a deposit that confirmed on-chain but wasn't reflected in the app. The 10-minute cron gap plus Arbitrum bridge latency produces an opaque wait with no status feedback.

**Suggested action:** (1) Show deposit status in the UI — on-chain confirmed, bridge in progress, arrived on Hyperliquid. (2) Consider a "manual sweep" button or webhook-trigger for faster pickup. (3) Pre-flight the cron to a shorter window for new users' first 24h. Longer-term: a fiat-to-HL direct path (even a how-to guide) would reduce dependency on Arbitrum bridging.

**What's happening:** The deposit notification reached Taiwo roughly 20 minutes after the funds confirmed. The cron architecture (10-min sweep, separate agent registration cron) creates cascading delays. The notification arriving late is not just a UX annoyance: the user has just sent real money and has no confirmation the platform received it.

**Why it matters:** Late money notifications erode trust at the highest-stakes moment in onboarding. Taiwo reacted with "Oh my money" and visible concern at 00:19:35. A user without Henry to say "it's on chain, don't worry" would have real anxiety.

**Suggested action:** Investigate why this notification was delayed (the deposit monitor / cron timing chain). Consider a faster confirmation path, even a webhook from Arbitrum event rather than polling. At minimum, show on-chain deposit status in the app so users have a confirmation path that doesn't depend on the notification arriving.

### Plan / Pricing

#### [MED · Copy / UI] Two layers of tier gating surprise the user separately

> "Why are you restricting access to some of these assets? You already tell me I can only have two positions and now you're telling me that there's also a limit to what I can actually..." (00:35:28)

**What's happening:** Taiwo discovered the 2-position cap during the plan selection phase. He then separately discovered that certain assets are also locked behind the plan. These feel like two separate "gotchas" rather than one coherent description of what Standard includes. The asset-level gating became visible when he accidentally clicked a locked asset and was redirected to an upgrade prompt.

**Why it matters:** Tier restrictions that surface as surprises mid-session undermine trust. The user feels they are being nickel-and-dimed after already paying. Both limits should be fully understood before the payment is processed.

**Suggested action:** The plan selection card should enumerate both constraints together: "2 active positions · Access to 3 assets (BTC, Apple, Nvidia + S&P 500) · Full automation available." No reveals post-payment.

### Home

#### [MED · Copy / UI] Signal BUY/WAIT/SELL meaning requires verbal explanation

> "So this weight for example, what does it" (00:38:29)
> "If I was holding Ethereum at this point, would it tell me to sell?" (00:39:32)
> Henry: "No, it's not saying buy or sell." (00:39:32)

**What's happening:** Taiwo saw the signal indicators and could not interpret them without Henry's help. Henry had to explain all three states: BUY = system is nudging a long trade, WAIT = price normalizing / direction unclear, and the relationship between "WAIT" and "SELL" for someone holding an asset. The UI minimized technical detail but went too far: the core meaning of the three states is not readable.

**Why it matters:** The signal list is the main content surface. If users cannot interpret what they are seeing, the page delivers no value and reinforces confusion about what the platform does.

**Suggested action:** Add inline tooltip or legend: "BUY: system sees a long trade setup. WAIT: conditions unclear, no action. SELL: system sees a short trade setup." One sentence per state, visible without a click.
